\chapter{Discussion}

\section{Validation of the Dragonfly Landing Site}
\label{sec:dragonfly_validation}

\subsection{Mission Science Objectives}
\label{sec:dragonfly_objectives}

NASA's \dragonfly{} mission \citep{Lorenz2018Dragonfly}, selected as the
fourth New Frontiers programme mission in 2019 and due to arrive at \titan{}
in 2034, is a dual-quadcopter rotorcraft that exploits \titan{}'s dense
atmosphere ($\rho \approx 5.4$\,kg\,m$^{-3}$) and low surface gravity to
achieve powered flight.  The baseline mission covers approximately 175\,km
of traverse over a 2.7-year nominal lifetime, beginning in the Belet dune
field roughly 50\,km north-west of Selk crater and progressing through the
crater ejecta blanket to the crater floor.  The three primary science
objectives are: (1) characterisation of the organic chemical inventory at
multiple surface locations and assessment of prebiotic potential; (2)
measurement of the energy sources, chemical gradients, and biosignature
potential at the Selk crater site; and (3) investigation of the geological
record of past liquid-water environments in the crater ejecta and floor.
The instrument suite --- DraMS (mass spectrometer), DraGNS (gamma-ray and
neutron spectrometer), DragonCam (camera suite), and DraGMet (meteorology
and geophysics including the EFIELD electric-field experiment) --- directly
addresses these objectives through chemical analysis, subsurface hydrogen
sensing, and contextual geological imaging.

\subsection{Two Distinct Habitability Frameworks}
\label{sec:two_frameworks}

Before evaluating the pipeline's results against the \dragonfly{} site
selection, it is essential to distinguish two fundamentally different
scientific frameworks for assessing \titan{}'s habitability, which
produce different site rankings and address different mission objectives.

\paragraph{Present-day solvent habitability.}
This framework asks: \textit{where does liquid solvent exist at the present
epoch that could support metabolic chemistry?}  For water-based life, the
answer is the inaccessible subsurface ocean.  For non-aqueous hydrocarbon
biochemistry \citep{McKaySmith2005}, the answer is the north polar
methane--ethane lakes.  The Bayesian pipeline in this thesis implements
\textit{present-day solvent habitability}: the eight features weight liquid
hydrocarbon availability ($w_1 = 0.25$), methane cycle activity
($w_4 = 0.15$), and surface--atmosphere interaction ($w_5 = 0.08$) — all
present-day physical processes — producing maps that correctly identify
polar lake shores as the globally highest-scoring environments
($P(H) \approx 0.44$ at the present epoch; Kraken southern shore).

\paragraph{Prebiotic chemistry habitability.}
This framework asks: \textit{where has liquid water most plausibly
contacted organic material on geologically relevant timescales?}
\citet{Neish2018} identified large impact craters as the clear answer.
The thermal energy of a major impact melts the water-ice crust, producing
transient liquid water that remains liquid for $10^3$--$10^6$\,yr at
temperatures that accelerate chemical reaction rates by several orders of
magnitude over ambient conditions \citep{Artemieva2003, OBrien2005}.
Water-ice mixed with \titan{}'s photolysed tholin organics under these
conditions is expected to produce amino acids and nucleobase precursors
via Strecker synthesis \citep{Neish2010}.  Selk, Menrva, and Sinlap are
the largest confirmed fresh craters on \titan{} and the primary
prebiotic chemistry targets by this framework.  This is the scientific
framework within which \dragonfly{} was selected: the mission explicitly
targets \textit{evidence of past water--organic contact chemistry}, not
present-day liquid \citep{Lorenz2021Landing}.

\paragraph{Why the two frameworks give different site rankings.}
The polar hydrocarbon lakes rank high under present-day solvent
habitability because they contain persistent surface liquid.  They rank
\textit{lower} under the prebiotic chemistry framework precisely because
liquid methane and ethane are non-polar: they do not dissolve the ionic
species required for Strecker synthesis, and they never contact the
water-ice crust in a chemically productive way.
Conversely, impact craters rank low under present-day solvent habitability
(no present liquid at the equatorial surface) but high under prebiotic
chemistry habitability (confirmed past liquid water).  \citet{Neish2018}
explicitly concluded that the polar lakes are \textit{not} the best places
to search for biosignatures, because the non-polar solvent cannot drive the
relevant prebiotic chemistry.

This distinction is not a limitation of either framework: both are
scientifically valid answers to different questions.  The implication for
this thesis is that the pipeline's low present-epoch score for
Selk ($P(H) = 0.24$) is not a contradiction of the \dragonfly{} mission's rationale
--- it is a \textit{consequence} of targeting different habitability.
Dragonfly was sent to Selk because Selk scored low on present-day liquid
but high on past water--organic contact.

\subsection{Quantitative Validation of Site Selection}
\label{sec:dragonfly_quant}

Within the framework of Section~\ref{sec:two_frameworks}, the pipeline
provides a meaningful independent validation of the \dragonfly{} landing
site, but through a different route than a global ranking.

The pipeline is a present-day solvent habitability model.  By this
metric, the north polar lake margins are the most habitable environments
on \titan{}, reaching $P(H) \approx 0.44$ (Kraken southern shore) and
$0.43$ (Ligeia eastern margin) --- the highest values in the present-epoch
map.  Selk scores $P(H) = 0.24$, below the prior mean of $0.33$,
because the crater lacks present-day surface liquid ($f_1 \approx 0.05$)
and its methane-cycle and surface--atmosphere scores are equatorial-low.
\textit{This is scientifically correct and consistent with Dragonfly's
mission rationale}: a model that correctly captures present-day solvent
habitability should rank polar lake shores above a dry equatorial crater.

The independent validation of Dragonfly's site selection comes from
the geomorphological diversity feature.  Selk's $f_7 = 0.629$
is the highest value of any equatorial site and 7.8$\times$ the global
median of 0.092 --- a result derived from Cassini SAR terrain data
alone, without reference to the mission planning process, and without
any weight, feature specification, or backfill strategy adjusted after
examining Selk.  The Bayesian posterior at Selk ($P(H) = 0.24$)
is correctly interpreted as: this site has below-average present-day
solvent habitability but anomalously high terrain-class diversity,
encoding the organic--water-ice interface that is the scientific target
of the mission.  Geomorphological diversity captures the simultaneous
presence of four terrain classes (dune, plains, labyrinth, crater floor)
within 45\,km, which is the Cassini-era observational proxy for the
organic--water-ice mixing interface that defines the prebiotic chemistry
target.  The pipeline independently identifies Selk as the equatorial
site with the highest terrain-class heterogeneity --- which is precisely
the physical attribute cited in the \dragonfly{} science rationale
\citep{Lorenz2021Landing, Neish2018}.

The model therefore validates not Selk's global present-day habitability
rank (which is unremarkable) but the \textit{specific physical reasoning}
behind the site selection: the organic--water-ice boundary that is
accessible there and nowhere else among reachable equatorial sites.

The polar lake margins represent a complementary, not competing, science
target.  A future mission targeting present-day habitability --- such as
the POSEIDON lake lander concept \citep{Tosi2022POSEIDON} --- would be
directly guided by the polar-lake-dominant result of this pipeline.  The
two mission concepts, \dragonfly{} and a future lake lander, address the
two habitability frameworks in sequence: \dragonfly{} tests the prebiotic
chemistry of past liquid-water environments; a lake mission tests
present-day hydrocarbon-solvent habitability.  This thesis provides the
quantitative global habitability map that motivates the latter.

The DraMS instrument is the most directly relevant for model calibration.
If DraMS measures amino acid concentrations at both the crater floor and
the Belet dune field, the ratio of these concentrations provides a direct
test of the organic abundance feature at sub-pixel resolution.  The model
assigns dune terrain an organic score of 0.82 versus 0.35 for crater terrain,
predicting that the dune field has higher organic abundance per unit volume.
However, the crater floor may have higher diversity of molecular species
due to impact processing and water-ice mixing, even if bulk organic
concentration is lower.  A discrepancy --- for instance, if the crater
floor is found to be richer in complex prebiotic molecules than the dune
field despite lower bulk tholin content --- would provide specific guidance
for revising the organic abundance scoring for sediment-filled crater floors,
directly improving the model for future analysis.

The DraGNS hydrogen-abundance measurement tests the water-ice interpretation
of the SAR-bright annulus and ejecta blanket.  The model predicts elevated
water-ice content in the ejecta relative to the adjacent dune sand,
consistent with excavation of ice-bearing crust.  Confirmation of this
signature validates the geomorphological classification of crater terrain
and the subsurface ocean proximity score at Selk.

The EFIELD experiment aboard DraGMet, designed to detect Schumann resonances
in \titan{}'s electromagnetic cavity, will attempt to constrain the ice
shell thickness and hence the ocean depth \citep{Lagouanelle2024}.  A tighter
constraint on ocean depth improves the physical basis of the $f_8$ prior:
a shallow ocean ($<$40\,km) significantly increases the plausibility of
past surface--interior liquid exchange, whilst a deep ocean ($>$80\,km)
would reduce the probability that the SAR-bright annuli represent
ocean-connected features.

\section{Biosignature Framework}
\label{sec:biosignatures}

\subsection{The Detection Challenge}
\label{sec:biosig_challenge}

Interpreting potential biosignatures on \titan{} is complicated by the
uncertain nature of the target biochemistry.  On Mars or Enceladus, the
presumed biochemistry would be water-based and phylogenetically related
to common terrestrial organisms, making standard isotopic and molecular
biosignatures directly applicable.  On \titan{}'s surface, three distinct
chemistry regimes are relevant to different epochs and locations: abiotic
Strecker synthesis in ancient impact melts (water-based but prebiotic);
acetylenotrophy in present-day hydrocarbon lake margins (potentially biotic
but non-aqueous); and future red-giant ocean chemistry (water-based,
aqueous).  Each regime has different biosignature targets, and the
\dragonfly{} mission primarily addresses the first two.

Following the framework of \citet{Catling2018}, a credible biosignature
requires context-dependence (interpretable against background abiotic
chemistry), reproducibility, and resistance to false positives.  Three
measurement categories satisfy these criteria for \titan{}.

\subsection{Enantiomeric Excess}
\label{sec:enantiomers}

Abiotic synthesis, including the Strecker reaction, produces racemic
(equal L/D) amino acid mixtures.  Terrestrial life produces exclusively
L-amino acids.  A statistically significant enantiomeric excess in DraMS
amino acid measurements at Selk would be a strong biosignature.  DraMS
has demonstrated sensitivity to amino acid enantiomeric ratios for
compounds expected at \titan{} \citep{Brinckerhoff2019}.  However, small
excesses ($<$10\%) can arise abiotically from asymmetric UV photodestruction
or selective mineral surface adsorption; a biosignature interpretation
requires excesses $\gg$10\%, reproducibility across multiple traverse
waypoints, and consistency with a self-replicating biochemical system.

\subsection{Carbon Isotopic Fractionation}
\label{sec:isotopes}

Biological carbon fixation preferentially incorporates $^{12}$C over
$^{13}$C, producing $\delta^{13}$C depletions of $-25$ to $-30$\,\textperthousand{}
(VPDB) in primary production organics on Earth.  Abiotic tholin synthesis
produces a distinct fractionation pattern driven by the kinetic isotope
effect in UV photolysis.  DraMS can measure organic carbon isotopic ratios;
a $\delta^{13}$C anomaly at the biological-range magnitude, reproducible
at multiple sites along the traverse and correlated with the model's
highest-scoring terrain, would constitute a strong biosignature.

\subsection{Acetylene Depletion}
\label{sec:acetylenedep}

If acetylenotrophy (Equation~\ref{eq:acetylenotrophy}) is active at the
lake-margin environments identified as the globally highest-scoring in the
present epoch, the surface \ce{C2H2} abundance should be measurably below
photochemical model predictions \citep{Vuitton2007}.  The anomalous downward
\ce{H2} flux reported by \citet{Strobel2010} is consistent with this
hypothesis.  \citet{Yanez2024} estimated a predicted depletion of 5--15\%
below photochemical equilibrium for acetylenotrophy at a metabolic energy
density comparable to the most energy-sparse known terrestrial organisms ---
within DraMS's projected sensitivity.  Crucially, the model identifies
lake-margin terrain (not the Selk crater) as the highest-scoring environment
for this biosignature, implying that a future mission directly targeting
the north polar shorelines would be the most powerful test of the
acetylenotrophy hypothesis.

\subsection{Interpreting a Null Result}
\label{sec:null_result}

A null biosignature result at Selk would constrain specifically the
``preserved impact-melt Strecker chemistry'' pathway at that location and
epoch.  It would not constrain: (1) acetylenotrophy at the north polar lake
margins, which score 15--20 percentage points higher than Selk in the present
epoch and which \dragonfly{} does not visit; (2) life in the subsurface
ocean, insensitive to surface sampling; or (3) extinct life from the LHB
whose signatures may be buried below accessible depth.  The habitability maps
provide a rigorous framework for reporting a null result in terms of which
specific pathways have been tested and which remain open.

\section{Titan in Solar System Habitability Context}
\label{sec:solar_context}

Figure~\ref{fig:solar_comparison} places \titan{}'s present-epoch and future
habitability scores in the context of the principal candidate habitable
environments in the solar system.  Two features distinguish \titan{} from all
other candidates.  First, it is the only body with a confirmed cycling surface
solvent in contact with a large organic inventory at the present epoch:
Enceladus has confirmed hydrothermal activity but essentially no surface organic
stockpile; Europa and Ceres have subsurface water but neither confirmed organics
nor a surface solvent.  Second, future \titan{} uniquely combines a global
water-based ocean with an organic feedstock accumulated over $\sim$16\,Gyr ---
a combination unprecedented elsewhere in the solar system.

\section{Comparison with the Affholder et al.\ (2021) Enceladus Framework}
\label{sec:affholder_comparison}

The closest methodological precedent for this work is \citet{Affholder2021},
who applied Bayesian inference to Cassini INMS plume data from Enceladus
to compare the posterior probabilities of abiotic serpentinisation versus
biological methanogenesis.  Their conclusion --- that the observed methane
escape rate is most parsimoniously explained by methanogenesis if the prior
probability of life exceeds $\sim$0.35 --- represents the most rigorous
quantitative biosignature assessment published for any solar system body.

The present work extends the Affholder et al.\ approach in three dimensions.
It is spatial, producing a 6.49-million-pixel posterior map rather than a
single-point estimate.  It is multi-feature, combining eight independent
observational proxies rather than a single plume composition measurement.
And it is multi-epoch, addressing three qualitatively distinct habitability
regimes across geological time.

A key conceptual difference also warrants emphasis.  \citet{Affholder2021}
compute $P(\text{life}\mid\text{plume data})$ --- the posterior probability
that life exists, given chemical measurements --- whilst this work computes
$P(\text{habitable}\mid\featurevec)$ --- the probability that the environment
is suitable for life.  This distinction, articulated by \citet{Cockell2014}
in the concept of ``uninhabited but habitable'' environments, matters
practically: a habitable environment is a necessary but not sufficient
condition for life.  The present analysis identifies where to look; the
Affholder-style framework tests, once measurements are made, whether life
is actually present.  The two approaches are complementary and sequential:
habitability mapping prioritises targets, in-situ chemistry quantifies
whether those targets are actually inhabited.

The recent results of \citet{Affholder2025} demonstrating thermodynamic
viability of glycine fermentation in \titan{}'s subsurface ocean provide
an important constraint on the subsurface ocean proximity feature ($f_8$).
If the ocean is indeed habitable, then surface evidence of past
subsurface--surface connectivity (the SAR-bright annuli at Selk and other
crater sites) gains additional scientific weight.  A future framework
integrating the Affholder (2025) ocean habitability posterior with the
present surface habitability maps, propagated through a physical model
of subsurface--surface transport, would represent a significant advance.


\section{Temporal Evolution of Habitable Environments}
\label{sec:temporal_evolution}

\subsection{Overview: Three Regime Transitions}
\label{sec:temporal_overview}

The top-10 habitability rankings (Table~\ref{tab:top10_past}--\ref{tab:top10_future})
reveal that \titan{}'s habitability landscape is not static but passes through
three qualitatively distinct regimes over the $\sim$10\,Gyr span considered.

\subsection{Regime 1: Impact-Melt and Cryovolcanic Dominance ($-3.8$ to $-1.5$\,Gya)}
\label{sec:regime_past}

During the Late Heavy Bombardment the absence of polar lakes means that the
liquid-hydrocarbon feature $f_1$ contributes near zero everywhere.  Instead,
the top-10 list is dominated by two site types: large impact craters and
candidate cryovolcanic features (Hotei Regio, Sotra Facula).

Impact craters score highly because the PAST-epoch model activates the
impact-melt proximity feature ($w{=}0.10$, $\mu{=}0.50$), encoding the
transient water--ammonia melt ponds described by \citet{OBrien2005} and
\citet{Artemieva2003}.  Menrva (392\,km diameter) leads the crater class
with $P(H){=}0.33$ because its large diameter implies a greater melt volume
and longer liquid persistence.  Selk (90\,km) follows at $P(H){=}0.31$,
positioned at the organic-richest terrain boundary.  All craters are
\textit{land sites} (squares in Table~\ref{tab:top10_past}): there is no
confirmed surface liquid at this epoch.

Cryovolcanic sites (Hotei Regio $P(H){=}0.295$; Sotra Facula $P(H){=}0.289$)
lead the PAST ranking because they combine elevated subsurface ocean proximity
($f_8(t){\approx}0.30$--$0.35$ after $\times$2.5 LHB scaling) with high
surface--atmosphere interaction, representing potential water--ammonia
conduits from the subsurface ocean to the surface.
The globally elevated acetylene energy ($s_3{\approx}2.1{\times}$) from the
young Sun also boosts equatorial organic-seed sites (Belet, Shangri-La)
into the top-3, co-ranking them with the proto-polar-basin sites.

\paragraph{Key change from PRESENT.}  The polar lake shore sites (ranks 1--6
in PRESENT) drop to ranks 6--8 in PAST because $f_1{=}0$ (no polar seas),
$f_4$ is suppressed (methane cycle uncertain at early epoch), and $f_5$
is redistributed toward cryovolcanic features.  Equatorial organic-rich dunes
also fall because organic accumulation was only $\sim$30\% of the present-day
inventory ($\mu_2 = 0.30$ vs 0.70 at PRESENT).

\subsection{Regime 2: Polar-Lake Dominance ($-1.0$\,Gya to $+4.0$\,Gya)}
\label{sec:regime_present}

The consolidation of Kraken, Ligeia, and Punga Mares around $-1.0\,\gya$
produces a step-change in the top-10 rankings.  By the LAKE\_FORMATION epoch
six of the top ten sites are polar lake shores; by the PRESENT epoch all six
polar/southern lake sites occupy ranks 1--6.

The physical mechanism driving this transition is threefold.
First, the liquid hydrocarbon feature $f_1$ rises from near zero
(PAST: $s_1{=}0.10$) to unity at confirmed lake pixels, contributing
$0.25 \times 1.0 = 0.25$ to the weighted feature sum at maximum.
Second, the methane cycle feature $f_4$ is highest ($\approx 0.70$) at
the north polar margins, reflecting the GCM-derived storm-track distribution.
Third, the shoreline environment scores high on geodiversity $f_7$ (multiple
terrain classes within 45\,km) and surface--atmosphere interaction $f_5$
(lake margins, channel inflow, seasonal wetting--drying).

The named north polar lacus (Jingpo, Mackay, Koitere, Neagh, Bolsena; ranks 5--10 in PRESENT) represent a persistent
secondary cluster throughout this regime, driven by high organic abundance
$f_2 \approx 0.82$ (VIMS-confirmed tholin dunes).  Their position below
the polar sites reflects the absence of surface liquid: the model correctly
ranks environments with both solvent \textit{and} organic substrate
above those with organics alone.

Ontario Lacus (72\textdegree{}S) occupies rank 5 in PRESENT despite being
$27\times$ smaller than Kraken Mare.  It scores below the north polar sites
primarily because the methane cycle is suppressed at the south pole during the
Cassini epoch's northern summer ($f_4{\approx}0.45$ vs 0.70 at Kraken), and
the lake is actively shrinking \citep{Turtle2011b}.

The NEAR\_FUTURE epoch ($+0.25\,\gya$) is analytically identical to PRESENT
(spatial correlation $r{>}0.999$): the $+1.4\%$ solar luminosity increase
has negligible effect on any feature, and all rankings are preserved.
This stability persists until approximately $+4.0\,\gya$, when solar warming
begins to evaporate the polar seas.

\subsection{Regime 3: Global Ocean ($+5.1$ to $+6.0$\,Gya)}
\label{sec:regime_future}

The water--ammonia eutectic crossing at $+5.1\,\gya$ (when $T_s \geq 176\,\mathrm{K}$)
marks the most dramatic habitability regime change.  The entire feature set
is replaced: the eight present-epoch features give way to water--ammonia
solvent ($w{=}0.30$), organic stockpile ($w{=}0.20$), dissolved chemical
energy ($w{=}0.15$), and water--ammonia cycle ($w{=}0.10$).

The global prior mean rises to $\mu_0{=}0.67$, reflecting the global scale
of the ocean habitat.  The top-10 list is fundamentally reorganised:
former lake basins (Kraken, Ligeia, Punga) continue to score highest
($P(H){=}0.76$--0.76) because they are the deepest bathymetric features
and thus have the most concentrated dissolved organics and strongest thermal
stratification.  However, equatorial dune fields now enter the top-10
at ranks 6--10 ($P(H){\approx}0.71$): the billions of years of accumulated
tholin organics, previously unreactive at 94\,K, are now dissolved in
$\sim$200\,K water--ammonia and available as chemical substrate.  This
represents the single largest increase in equatorial habitability in the
entire reconstruction.

\paragraph{Why former lake basins lead.}
The high Bayesian scores at Kraken and Ligeia in the FUTURE epoch reflect
three factors: (1) the organic stockpile feature $f_{\mathrm{org}}$ is highest
where organic accumulation was greatest (polar basins received methane-cycle
deposition for billions of years); (2) the dissolved energy feature
$f_{\mathrm{dis}}$ scales with accumulated organic inventory; (3) the global ocean
habitability prior $f_{\mathrm{glo}}$ is elevated near basin floors, encoding
the Affholder et al.\ (2021) result that the subsurface ocean is
thermodynamically habitable.

\subsection{The Habitable Minimum at $+5.0$\,Gya: An Astrobiological Desert}
\label{sec:habitable_minimum}

Between approximately $+4.0\,\gya$ (when polar seas evaporate) and
$+5.1\,\gya$ (when the eutectic is first crossed), \titan{} is transiently
solvent-free.  The global Bayesian posterior drops to its minimum
($P(H) \approx \mu_0^\text{NEAR\_FUTURE} \approx 0.33$, with all liquid
features zeroed), and no site scores above $\sim$0.28.  This is the one
epoch at which neither hydrocarbon-solvent nor water-based habitability
is available simultaneously.  The duration is $\sim$100\,Myr --- brief by
geological standards but long by biological ones.  Any life that had
established itself in the polar lakes would face extinction pressure at
this epoch unless it adopted dormancy or subsurface refugia.

\section{Limitations of the Framework}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Conditional Feature Independence}
\label{sec:independence_limit}

The Bayesian model treats the eight features as conditionally independent
given the habitability latent variable $H$.  This assumption simplifies the
posterior update to a closed-form Beta distribution but introduces potential
bias where features are correlated.  In particular, organic abundance ($f_2$)
and chemical energy ($f_3$) are positively correlated: both are elevated over
tholin-rich organic terrain.  Liquid hydrocarbon ($f_1$) and surface--
atmosphere interaction ($f_5$) are structurally correlated through the
lake-margin component of $f_5$.  Under positive correlation, the posterior
is slightly overestimated relative to a fully specified multivariate model.
The magnitude of this bias can be estimated by comparing the independent-feature
posterior with a correlated Gaussian copula model; preliminary assessment
suggests the bias is $<$0.03 in posterior mean, smaller than the inter-epoch
differences and the 95\% HDI width.  A full multivariate Bayesian model using
a Gaussian copula structure is a natural extension for future work.

\subsection{Static Geomorphological Template}
\label{sec:static_template}

The Lopes et al.\ (2019) geomorphological map is used for all five temporal
configurations and all 72 interpolated epochs.  In reality, the surface
at $-3.5\gya$ had substantially different geomorphology: fewer confirmed
dunes (the tholin inventory was smaller), different crater density (many
present-day craters did not yet exist, and many LHB craters may have been
subsequently buried or eroded), and no polar lakes.  The future surface
will be partially or completely submerged in the red-giant ocean.  The use
of the present-day map as a proxy for all epochs is an unavoidable
simplification given the absence of independent temporal geomorphological
information, and represents the largest qualitative uncertainty in the
temporal analysis.

\subsection{The Past-Epoch Cassini Contamination}
\label{sec:past_contamination}

As documented in Appendix~\ref{app:assumptions} (A4), the PAST epoch
posterior was computed using present-era Cassini SAR maps, which encode
the present polar lake morphology even at $-3.5\gya$.  The resulting inflation of the north polar Bayesian score above its
expected prior value is mitigated in the multi-epoch reconstruction
by using the analytical Bayesian formula directly for pre-lake-formation
frames rather than the contaminated PAST anchor.
However, the base-level inflation persists in the PAST static map.
Readers should note that Table~\ref{tab:craters} reports crater scores
from the PAST anchor, and these crater scores are not affected by the
polar contamination (the impact-melt feature is spatially uncorrelated
with the lake pattern).  The north polar PAST score of $\sim$0.70 is,
however, an artefact and should be interpreted with caution.

\section{Sensitivity of Results to Prior Specification}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

A natural concern with any Bayesian analysis is whether the conclusions
depend sensitively on the prior specification.  Three parameters govern
the prior in this framework: $\kappa$ (prior concentration), $\lambda$
(likelihood sharpness), and the feature weights $w_i$ (particularly
$w_1 = 0.25$ for liquid hydrocarbon, the largest single weight).
Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} shows the result of varying each parameter
at three representative sites: Kraken southern shore (rank 1, high
liquid hydrocarbon), Belet dune field (rank 11, outside top-10 at present),
and Selk crater (below prior mean, high geodiversity).

The key finding is robustness.  Halving $\kappa$ from 5 to 2.5
increases Kraken's $P(H)$ by $+0.031$ and reduces Selk's by $-0.027$;
doubling $\kappa$ to 10 reverses these changes.  The maximum shift
across all parameter variants tested is $|{\Delta}P(H)| = 0.033$ --- well
below the 95\% HDI width of $\approx 0.5$ at any single site.  The
qualitative ranking (polar lake shores $>$ north polar lacus $>$
equatorial organic sites) is preserved in all $3 \times 3 \times 3 = 27$
combinations tested.  Crucially, reducing $w_1$ from 0.25 to 0.15 (halving the weight
of liquid hydrocarbon) only reduces Kraken's $P(H)$ from 0.436 to 0.420
and raises Belet's from 0.295 to 0.319 --- the ranking order is unchanged.
This demonstrates that the result (polar lake shores dominate) is not an
artefact of assigning liquid hydrocarbon the highest weight; it is
driven by the coincidence of multiple high-scoring features at the
shoreline.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

\subsection{Post-Dragonfly Model Calibration}
\label{sec:postdragonfly}

The most important near-term improvement to the framework will come from
\dragonfly{} DraMS measurements at Selk crater and along the Belet--Selk
traverse.  Direct measurements of amino acid concentration and molecular
complexity at each terrain class visited will calibrate the organic abundance
scoring ($f_2$) at per-pixel resolution for dune, crater, and ejecta terrain
types.  These calibrated scores can be propagated globally via the Lopes
(2019) terrain classification, reducing the dominant uncertainty in the
organic abundance feature.  A post-\dragonfly{} version of the framework,
incorporating these calibration measurements, would produce substantially
tighter posterior distributions globally and would directly improve the
scientific value of any follow-on \titan{} orbital mission.

\subsection{Extension to a Full Multivariate Model}
\label{sec:multivariate}

The current Beta-conjugate model assumes feature independence.  A natural
extension is a multivariate Bayesian model using a Gaussian copula to
represent feature correlations, estimated from the within-class
co-variation in feature values across the global pixel population.  The
copula parameters can be estimated from the Cassini dataset directly,
without requiring additional observations, and would reduce the positive
bias associated with correlated positive features at lake margins.
Implementation using PyMC or NumPyro is straightforward within the existing
framework and would improve the credibility of the HDI estimates.

\subsection{Additional Temporal Anchor Epochs}
\label{sec:more_anchors}

The current five-anchor temporal reconstruction has the largest uncertainties
at the lake-formation transition ($-1.5$ to $-0.5\gya$) and the solar-warming
collapse ($+4.0$ to $+5.5\gya$).  These transitions are physically
important --- one represents the establishment of the present-day dominant
habitability regime, the other its collapse --- and are currently represented
by interpolation between widely-spaced anchors.  Adding three to five
additional anchor epochs concentrated at these transitions, generated by
running the full Bayesian pipeline with physically appropriate feature
sets at those intermediate times, would substantially improve the temporal
resolution and reduce the interpolation uncertainty.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This thesis has presented the first quantitative, spatially-resolved,
multi-epoch Bayesian habitability map of \titan{}'s surface, derived from
eight observational proxy features extracted from the complete Cassini
remote sensing dataset.  The conclusions are organised here directly by
the four research questions stated in Section~\ref{sec:scope}.

\paragraph{RQ1: Spatial distribution of surface habitability and feature validation.}
\textbf{North polar lake margins are the most habitable present-day environments.}
The southern shoreline of Kraken Mare achieves $P(H) \approx 0.44$, the
highest Bayesian posterior in the present-epoch map (Table~\ref{tab:top10_present},
rank 1).  The dominant drivers are coincident maximum liquid hydrocarbon
solvent ($f_1{=}1.0$), active methane cycle ($f_4{\approx}0.70$), and high
shoreline geodiversity ($f_7{\approx}0.76$).  Equatorial dune fields occupy
ranks 7--10 via high organic abundance ($f_2{\approx}0.82$) but are limited
by the absence of surface liquid.  All eight feature importance/weight
ratios lie within 10\% of unity (Table~\ref{tab:importances}), confirming
that the geographic discrimination provided by each Cassini observable
matches the astrobiological importance assigned through independent
physical reasoning.

\paragraph{RQ2: Late Heavy Bombardment habitability and impact structures.}
\textbf{During the LHB, impact-melt and cryovolcanic sites were primary.}
At $-3.5\,\gya$ the top-10 list is dominated by cryovolcanic features
(Hotei Regio $P(H){=}0.295$; Sotra Facula $P(H){=}0.289$);
cryovolcanic conduits lead because their elevated $f_8$ and $f_5$ combine
with the globally boosted acetylene proxy.  Craters (Menrva $P(H){\approx}0.27$;
Selk $P(H){\approx}0.26$) rank 11th and lower --- their advantage is captured
explicitly by the animation impact-melt bonus, not the 8-feature thesis formula
(Table~\ref{tab:top10_past}).
All top-10 LHB sites are land sites: no polar lakes exist at this epoch.
Selk's geological context at the Shangri-La dune--plains boundary
maximises the organic substrate available for Strecker amino acid
synthesis in the transient impact-melt pond, consistent with the
prebiotic chemistry framework of \citet{Neish2018}.

\paragraph{RQ3: Multi-epoch temporal trajectory and future habitability.}
\textbf{The temporal trajectory is non-monotonic, passing through three
qualitatively distinct regimes.}  (i)~LHB regime ($-3.8$ to
$-1.5\,\gya$): impact-melt and cryovolcanic dominance
(Section~\ref{sec:regime_past}).  (ii)~Polar-lake regime
($-1.0\,\gya$ to $+4.0\,\gya$): lake margins rank 1--6 with stable
rankings across LAKE\_FORMATION, PRESENT, and NEAR\_FUTURE epochs
(Tables~\ref{tab:top10_lakeform}--\ref{tab:top10_nearfuture}).
(iii)~Global-ocean regime ($+5.1$ to $+6.0\,\gya$): the FUTURE epoch
(Table~\ref{tab:top10_future}) sees a global reorganisation as all sites
score above $P(H){>}0.70$; former lake basins lead ($P(H){\approx}0.76$)
and equatorial dune fields remain in the top-6 (as they were at the PAST epoch) while lake shore sites begin to enter ranks 7--10 as their
vast organic stockpile becomes dissolved substrate.  Between regimes (ii)
and (iii) a transient astrobiological minimum occurs at $\sim+5.0\,\gya$
when \titan{} is briefly solvent-free (Section~\ref{sec:habitable_minimum}).
The red-giant ocean at $+5.9\,\gya$, maintained for $\sim$800\,Myr over a
$\sim$70\,km accumulated organic layer, represents an extraordinary future
habitability scenario unmatched elsewhere in the solar system.

\paragraph{RQ4: Independent validation of the Dragonfly landing site.}
\textbf{The Dragonfly site is independently validated through geomorphological
diversity.}  Selk crater has the highest geodiversity of any equatorial site
($f_7{=}0.629$, 7.8$\times$ the global median of 0.092), derived from Cassini
SAR terrain data without reference to the mission planning process.
This terrain-boundary diversity, encoding the simultaneous presence of dune,
plains, labyrinth, and crater-floor terrain within 45\,km, is the Cassini-era
observational proxy for the organic--water-ice mixing interface that is the
scientific target of the Dragonfly prebiotic chemistry mission.
Although Selk ranks below the polar lake shores in present-day solvent
habitability ($P(H){=}0.24$), this is scientifically consistent: Dragonfly
targets \textit{past} liquid-water chemistry, not present-day liquid, and
the framework correctly identifies the relevant distinguishing feature
(Section~\ref{sec:two_frameworks}).  The instrument--feature mapping
(Table~\ref{tab:instruments}) provides directly testable predictions for
DraMS, DraGNS, and EFIELD measurements at Selk.

\bigskip
Together, these results demonstrate that \titan{} possesses a multilayered
astrobiological significance extending from its geological past through the
present to the far future, and that its habitability can be quantified
spatially and temporally from the Cassini legacy dataset with sufficient
precision to guide mission planning and interpret in-situ measurements.
The Bayesian multi-feature framework and the top-10 site rankings presented
here provide a reproducible, extendable foundation for this work.
