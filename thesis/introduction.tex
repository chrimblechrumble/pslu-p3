\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{The Search for Habitable Environments Beyond Earth}
\label{sec:search_context}

The question of whether life exists elsewhere in the universe has undergone a
profound transformation over the past three decades, shifting from the domain
of philosophical speculation to empirical scientific investigation.  This
change has been driven by two largely independent but mutually reinforcing
developments: the systematic discovery that life on Earth thrives in conditions
previously considered inimical to biology, and the growing recognition through
robotic spacecraft exploration that chemically and thermodynamically suitable
environments for life are far more widely distributed throughout the solar
system than twentieth-century intuitions suggested.

The progressive characterisation of terrestrial extremophile communities has
fundamentally expanded the envelope of conditions considered compatible with
life.  Microorganisms have been cultured from deep-sea hydrothermal vents at
temperatures exceeding 121\textdegree{}C, from hypersaline brines with water
activities as low as $a_w = 0.60$, from the anoxic pore spaces of deep
crustal rocks where electrons liberated by water--rock reactions provide
metabolic energy in the total absence of sunlight, and from sub-glacial
Antarctic lakes that have been isolated from the atmosphere for millions of
years \citep{Rothschild2001, Cavicchioli2002}.  Each such discovery has
moved the habitable niche boundaries outwards, making it progressively more
difficult to argue that any particular planetary environment is categorically
uninhabitable without specific evidence that the relevant physical and
chemical conditions lie entirely outside the known envelope of life.

In parallel, the outer solar system has revealed a far richer array of
liquid environments than the classical habitable zone framework would predict.
The Europa detection from the Galileo mission \citep{Pappalardo1999} and the
subsequent discovery of active cryovolcanism and ocean chemistry at Enceladus
\citep{Waite2017} established that tidal heating can maintain liquid water
oceans beneath ice shells at heliocentric distances far beyond any reasonable
definition of the water-based habitable zone.  These discoveries shifted the
conceptual framework of solar system habitability from a two-dimensional
picture (terrestrial planets in a narrow orbital band) to a three-dimensional
one encompassing subsurface environments throughout the outer solar system.

Within this broader astrobiological context, Saturn's moon \titan{} occupies
a distinctive and arguably singular position.  It is the only moon in the
solar system possessing a substantial atmosphere --- one that is denser at the
surface than Earth's and dominated by molecular nitrogen, with a structurally
complex photochemistry driven by the ultraviolet flux of the distant Sun.
It is the only known extraterrestrial body to host stable surface liquids
\citep{Stofan2007}, completing a full precipitation-evaporation-lake cycle
that is the structural analogue of the terrestrial hydrological cycle.
It may have accumulated the most complex organic chemical inventory in the
solar system outside Earth itself \citep{Lorenz2008}.  And at depth, it
contains a water--ammonia ocean whose thermodynamic conditions are
increasingly well-constrained by tidal measurements and geophysical
modelling.  The combination of these properties makes \titan{} not
merely a potentially habitable world but a natural laboratory for
prebiotic chemistry operating at planetary scale and across a geological
timescale of $\sim$4.5\,Gyr.

\section{The Physical Environment of Titan}
\label{sec:titan_environment}

\subsection{Atmospheric Structure and Composition}
\label{sec:atmosphere}

\titan{}'s atmosphere was first confirmed as substantially nitrogen-rich by
radio occultation measurements from the Voyager\,1 flyby in November 1980,
which constrained the surface pressure to approximately 1.5\,bar and the
surface temperature to near 90\,K \citep{Tyler1981}.  The subsequent
\cassini{}--\huygens{} mission provided a comprehensive characterisation
that transformed our understanding of atmospheric structure.  The Huygens
Atmospheric Structure Instrument (HASI) measured a surface pressure of
$1.467\pm0.001$\,bar and a surface temperature of $93.65\pm0.25$\,K at
the probe's landing site (192.3\textdegree{}W, 10.6\textdegree{}S),
with a tropospheric lapse rate of $\sim$1.38\,K\,km$^{-1}$ and a
tropopause cold trap at $\sim$40\,km altitude where the temperature
reaches a minimum of $\sim$70\,K \citep{Fulchignoni2005}.  A
stratospheric temperature inversion produces a warmer mesosphere above,
analogous to the terrestrial stratosphere maintained by ozone absorption.

The \huygens{} Gas Chromatograph Mass Spectrometer (GCMS) measured a surface
composition of $\approx$94.2\% molecular nitrogen (\ce{N2}), $5.65\pm0.18$\%
methane (\ce{CH4}), trace argon-40 ($\approx$43\,ppm, consistent with
outgassing of $^{40}$K from the interior), and a variety of simple organic
molecules including cyanogen, acetonitrile, and propionitrile
\citep{Niemann2005}.  At higher altitudes, the Cassini INMS and CAPS
instruments revealed a far richer chemistry: complex nitriles, benzene,
and high-molecular-weight organic ions characteristic of nascent aerosol
formation are detected above $\sim$900\,km, confirming that the tholins
production process begins in the thermosphere and mesosphere and proceeds
downward through the stratosphere to the surface \citep{Waite2007}.

The methane cycle in \titan{}'s troposphere constitutes the dominant agent
of surface change and chemical transport.  Methane evaporates from the north
polar hydrocarbon seas, is transported equatorward by the global Hadley
circulation, condenses and precipitates as rainfall over equatorial and
mid-latitude terrain \citep{Turtle2011}, and drains back towards polar basins
through an extensive network of fluvial channels that \citet{Miller2021channels}
have mapped globally from Cassini SAR data.  The cycle operates on timescales
ranging from individual storm events of hours to decades, through the
29.5-year Saturnian seasonal cycle, to longer-period lake-level variations
driven by orbital forcing.  Its significance for habitability lies in the
continuous delivery of organic material from photochemically active equatorial
regions to the polar lake margins, and in the chemical gradients and
concentration cycles it produces at evaporating shorelines.

Ultraviolet photolysis of the \ce{N2}/\ce{CH4} mixture in the upper
atmosphere produces an extraordinary variety of complex organic photochemical
products collectively termed tholins \citep{Sagan1979}.  Laboratory synthesis
of tholins from \ce{N2}/\ce{CH4} mixtures under UV irradiation produces
hundreds of identifiable molecular species including simple nitriles, amino
acid precursors, nucleobase analogues, and sugar-like polyols \citep{Cable2012}.
The production rate of organic aerosols is estimated at
$\sim5\times10^{-14}$\,g\,cm$^{-2}$\,s$^{-1}$ \citep{Lavvas2011},
implying that the surface has accumulated an organic layer estimated at
between 1 and 10\,km depth over solar system history \citep{Lorenz2008}.
This organic reservoir is the primary chemical substrate for all habitability
scenarios considered in this thesis, whether in the present epoch (as
feedstock for acetylenotrophy), in the geologically recent past (as reactant
in impact-melt amino acid synthesis), or in the far future (as dissolved
substrate in the red-giant water--ammonia ocean).

\subsection{Surface Geology and Geomorphology}
\label{sec:geology}

The Cassini RADAR instrument mapped approximately 67\% of \titan{}'s surface
in synthetic aperture radar (SAR) mode at $\sim$350\,m per pixel resolution
over 127 targeted flybys spanning 2004--2017.  This coverage, whilst
substantial, leaves approximately one-third of the surface --- concentrated
in mid-southern latitudes and portions of the northern hemisphere --- dependent
on lower-sensitivity ISS and VIMS imagery alone.  The SAR dataset revealed a
geologically diverse world operating under conditions profoundly different from
any other solar system body: a geology dominated not by silicate tectonism
or basaltic volcanism but by organic sediment transport, cryovolcanism, and
the weathering and dissolution of ice by liquid hydrocarbon.

\citet{Lopes2019} synthesised the full Cassini SAR dataset into the first
comprehensive global geomorphological map of \titan{}, identifying seven terrain
classes that account for all mapped surface morphologies: plains ($\sim$65\%
of the SAR-imaged surface), dune fields ($\sim$17\%), mountains and hummocky
terrain ($\sim$8\%), basins ($\sim$3\%), labyrinth terrain ($\sim$4\%),
impact craters ($<$1\%), and polar hydrocarbon lakes and seas ($\sim$1.5\%)
\citep{Malaska2025}.  This geomorphological map forms a cornerstone of the
habitability framework developed in this thesis, both as a source of terrain
classification information for the organic abundance feature and as context
for interpreting the spatial patterns in the posterior habitability maps.

The equatorial dune seas --- Shangri-La, Belet, Aztlan, and Fensal, together
forming a near-continuous band from roughly $-30\textdegree$ to $+30\textdegree$
latitude --- are the single largest geomorphological unit on the planet.
These linear dunes, oriented consistent with eastward net sand transport by
the atmospheric circulation \citep{Tokano2010}, are composed of organic
particulates, most likely complex hydrocarbons and nitriles derived from
atmospheric tholin deposition \citep{Lorenz2006}.  Near-infrared spectroscopy
from VIMS confirms that dune terrain has the highest 1.59/1.27\,\textmu{}m
band ratio of any terrain class on \titan{}, indicative of the highest tholin
content \citep{LeMouelic2019}.  The dune belt accordingly receives the
highest organic abundance score (0.82) in the geomorphological classification
used here (Appendix~\ref{app:geo_scores}).

The north polar region above $\sim$60\textdegree{}N hosts the three major
hydrocarbon seas: Kraken Mare (area $\sim$400{,}000\,km$^2$, estimated maximum
depth $\sim$300\,m), Ligeia Mare ($\sim$126{,}000\,km$^2$, depth $\sim$200\,m),
and Punga Mare ($\sim$38{,}000\,km$^2$), together containing an estimated
$\sim$70{,}000\,km$^3$ of liquid \citep{Mastrogiuseppe2019}.  The compositions
of the two largest seas differ measurably.  Cassini active radar depth sounding
and microwave emissivity measurements indicate that Ligeia Mare is predominantly
methane-rich, whilst Kraken Mare is more ethane-rich (estimated at
approximately 70\% ethane, 16\% propane, 7\% methane, and 7\% nitrogen
by mole fraction near the 2004-era shoreline position), with composition
varying seasonally as methane preferentially evaporates \citep{Mastrogiuseppe2019}.
These chemical differences between adjacent sea bodies imply distinct chemical
environments at their respective shorelines and will be reflected in the
habitability mapping.

The Xanadu region ($\sim$90--150\textdegree{}W, equatorial) is anomalously
radar-bright in SAR and spectrally dominated by crystalline water ice in VIMS
measurements, in strong contrast to the organic-dominated signature of
surrounding terrain \citep{Brown2011}.  Its origin remains debated: proposed
explanations include excavation of water-ice-rich crustal material by a
large ancient impact \citep{Radebaugh2011}, a relict tectonic province, or
anomalous ice deposition.  Whatever its origin, Xanadu's water-ice-rich
composition results in a low organic abundance score and consequently low
habitability score in the present model.

Ontario Lacus at the south pole (179\textdegree{}W, 72\textdegree{}S) is the
only confirmed southern-hemisphere lake \citep{Turtle2009}, with an estimated
area of $\sim$15{,}000\,km$^2$.  ISS observations revealed measurable shoreline
recession between 2005 and 2010, suggesting net evaporation under southern
summer insolation at the Cassini epoch.  Its significantly smaller size and
lower associated methane cycle activity compared to the north polar seas means
it scores notably lower in the present-epoch habitability map.

\subsection{Interior Structure and the Subsurface Ocean}
\label{sec:interior}

The most critical interior constraint for habitability purposes is the tidal
Love number $k_2 = 0.589\pm0.150$ measured by \citet{Iess2012} from orbital
perturbations of the Cassini spacecraft during close flybys.  This value
greatly exceeds the prediction for a fully solid (rocky core plus solid ice
shell) body, for which $k_2 < 0.03$.  The only viable physical explanation
is a global subsurface liquid layer that deforms readily under Saturn's tidal
force, amplifying the tidal response.  Geophysical interior models
constrain this layer to a water--ammonia ocean at $\sim$55--80\,km depth,
beneath an outer ice-I shell and overlying an ice-VI mantle and silicate
core \citep{Tobie2005, Nimmo2010}.  The ocean's ammonia concentration is
estimated at 5--15\,wt\% from density constraints \citep{Mitri2014};
this concentration depresses the melting point of the mixture to near the
water--ammonia eutectic (176\,K at 32.6\,wt\% \ce{NH3}; \citealt{Grasset2005}),
ensuring the ocean remains liquid at its current depth despite surface
temperatures of only 94\,K.

The subsurface ocean has direct habitability relevance for two reasons.
First, \citet{Affholder2025} demonstrated that glycine fermentation
($\Delta G \approx -20$ to $-50$\,kJ/mol under estimated ocean conditions)
is energetically viable within the ocean, and that the dissolved organic
nitrogen available from hydrolysed tholins provides adequate substrate for
simple metabolic cycles.  Second, at the surface, SAR-bright annular features
surrounding several large impact craters are interpreted as ancient
melt-water fronts where liquid water sourced from the subsurface ocean (or
from impact melting) spread outward from crater basins, potentially bringing
ocean-derived chemistry into contact with the surface organic inventory
\citep{Wood2010}.  This interpretation underpins the elevated subsurface ocean
proximity score at Selk crater discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\section{Chemical Habitability of Titan's Surface: Prior Theoretical Work}
\label{sec:prior_theory}

\subsection{The Acetylenotrophy Hypothesis}
\label{sec:acetylenotrophy}

The first quantitative assessment of the energetic viability of life on
\titan{}'s surface was provided by \citet{McKaySmith2005}, who evaluated the
metabolic energy available from the combination of atmospheric acetylene
(\ce{C2H2}) with molecular hydrogen (\ce{H2}) --- a process they termed
acetylenotrophy:
\begin{equation}
  \ce{C2H2 + 3H2 -> 2CH4}, \qquad \Delta G = -334\,\text{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
  \label{eq:acetylenotrophy}
\end{equation}
Acetylene is produced continuously by ultraviolet photolysis of methane in
the upper atmosphere and sediments to the surface.  Molecular hydrogen, a
photochemical by-product, similarly settles downward.  McKay and Smith
calculated that the available acetylenotrophy energy flux at \titan{}'s
surface is comparable to that available from photosynthesis at the deep
ocean floor on Earth, and substantially exceeds the metabolic energy
requirements of the most energy-sparse known terrestrial organisms, including
methanogens and anaerobic chemoautotrophs.

Possible observational support for this hypothesis emerged from
\citet{Strobel2010}, who reported a downward flux of \ce{H2} to \titan{}'s
surface of $\sim10^{10}$\,molecules\,cm$^{-2}$\,s$^{-1}$ --- approximately
100 times the flux predicted by known abiotic surface reactions --- based on
analysis of Cassini INMS data.  A surface sink for \ce{H2} exceeding abiotic
predictions is precisely consistent with acetylenotrophy.  However,
\citet{Teolis2015} subsequently identified INMS calibration uncertainties at
low signal levels that could affect the reported flux magnitude, and the
measurement must be considered tentative pending confirmation by future
in-situ measurements.  The energetics of acetylenotrophy were revisited using
updated thermochemical data by \citet{Yanez2024}, who calculated a free
energy yield of 69--78\,kJ\,mol$^{-1}$\,C under \titan{} surface conditions
--- somewhat more conservative than the original McKay \& Smith estimate
but still comfortably above the minimum for terrestrial methanogens
($\sim$25--65\,kJ\,mol$^{-1}$\,C).

\subsection{Non-Aqueous Biochemistry and the Azotosome Hypothesis}
\label{sec:nonaqueous}

A complementary line of theoretical work has examined the possibility of
life operating with liquid methane as its biochemical solvent rather than
water.  \citet{Benner2004} provided a theoretical basis for non-aqueous
biochemistry, arguing that whilst water's polarity and hydrogen-bonding
capacity are indeed important for terrestrial biochemistry, these properties
are not thermodynamically fundamental requirements; a sufficiently different
but internally consistent biochemistry might evolve under different solvent
conditions.  In liquid methane at $\sim$90\,K, the principal structural
challenge is membrane formation: conventional phospholipid bilayers, whose
amphiphilic nature depends on the polarity contrast between water and
non-polar lipid tails, would not form spontaneously in a non-polar solvent.

\citet{Stevenson2015} addressed this challenge through molecular dynamics
simulations, showing that small nitrogen-containing molecules such as
acrylonitrile (\ce{CH2CHCN}), subsequently detected in \titan{}'s
stratosphere by ALMA \citep{Palmer2017}, can spontaneously form stable
membrane-like vesicles (termed azotosomes) in liquid methane.  These
azotosome membranes would have reversed polarity relative to terrestrial
cell membranes, with polar head groups directed inward towards a polar
interior.  Whilst no experimental demonstration of azotosome-based metabolism
exists, the thermodynamic and structural feasibility of membrane formation
establishes at least one plausible path to cellular compartmentalisation
in a methane-solvent biology.

\subsection{Prebiotic Chemistry in Post-Impact Melt Environments}
\label{sec:impact_prebiotic}

A third and distinctly different habitability pathway on \titan{} involves
transient liquid water rather than the permanent hydrocarbon lakes.  When
large impactors strike \titan{}'s ice-rich crust, the kinetic energy of
impact melts the surrounding material, producing water--ammonia--organic
mixture ponds that persist at liquid temperatures for $10^3$--$10^4$ years
before refreezing \citep{OBrien2005}.  For a 50\,km diameter rocky impactor
at \titan{}'s escape velocity of 2.64\,km\,s$^{-1}$, the delivered kinetic
energy is sufficient to melt $\sim10^4$\,km$^3$ of ice.  During the Late
Heavy Bombardment the impactor flux was approximately 100--1000 times the
present background rate, ensuring that multiple active melt pools existed
simultaneously on \titan{}'s surface at any time during the $\sim$300\,Myr
duration of the bombardment.

\citet{Madan2026} demonstrated through thermodynamic and kinetic modelling
that the Strecker reaction:
\begin{equation}
  \text{R-CHO} + \ce{NH3} + \ce{HCN}
  \xrightarrow{\;\text{Strecker}\;}
  \text{R-CH(NH}_2\text{)-COOH}
  \label{eq:strecker}
\end{equation}
is accessible in post-impact melt environments at temperatures of 200--350\,K.
All three precursors are available: surface aldehydes (\ce{R-CHO}) from tholin
decomposition, \ce{NH3} from the water--ammonia melt, and \ce{HCN} from the
nitrogen-rich atmosphere (HCN is one of the most abundant photochemical products
in \titan{}'s stratosphere; \citealt{Vuitton2007}).  The amino acid products
are stable on timescales comparable to the melt pool lifetime.  This pathway
is central to the astrobiological rationale for the \dragonfly{} mission's
primary landing site at Selk crater, where the post-impact melt chemistry
of an ancient large impact is preserved in the geological record.

\subsection{The Far-Future Habitability Window}
\label{sec:future_intro}

\citet{Lorenz1997} identified a fourth temporal window of classical
water-based habitability on \titan{} associated with the Sun's eventual
departure from the main sequence.  As solar luminosity increases by
factors of hundreds over $\sim10^7$--$10^8$\,yr during the red-giant
phase, \titan{}'s surface temperature will rise through the water--ammonia
eutectic (176\,K) and remain above it for an estimated 200--500\,Myr
\citep{Lorenz1997, Schroeder2008}.  During this window, \titan{}'s entire
surface would be covered by a global water--ammonia ocean, overlying an
organic sediment reservoir that by that epoch will have accumulated for
$\sim$16\,Gyr of continuous atmospheric deposition --- a chemical stockpile
of extraordinary richness.  In this scenario, \titan{} would become
transiently and potentially uniquely the most habitable body in the solar
system, at a time when Earth and Mars will have been sterilised by the
expanding solar photosphere.  The quantitative characterisation of this
future epoch is one of the novel contributions of this thesis.

\section{The Cassini--Huygens Mission and Its Astrobiological Legacy}
\label{sec:cassini_legacy}

The joint NASA/ESA \cassini{}--\huygens{} mission operated in the Saturnian
system from July 2004 until September 2017, when the spacecraft was
deliberately deorbited into Saturn's atmosphere to prevent biological
contamination of potentially habitable moons.  The 13-year operational
lifetime enabled 127 targeted \titan{} flybys, the \huygens{} probe descent
and landing in January 2005, and a systematic multi-instrument survey of
\titan{}'s atmosphere, surface, and interior that constitutes the observational
foundation of this thesis.

The observational coverage is comprehensive but incomplete.  SAR mapping
reached $\sim$67\% of the surface; the VIMS near-infrared spectral coverage
relevant to organic abundance assessment reaches approximately 50\%; the CIRS
thermal mapping achieved global coverage at $\sim$10\textdegree{} latitude
resolution; and the radar altimetry dataset covers $\sim$90\% of the globe
after global interpolation.  The GCMS aboard \huygens{} provided the single
direct measurement of surface chemical composition, at one equatorial site.
This pattern --- broad but spatially uneven remote sensing coverage combined
with a single point of in-situ ground truth --- defines both the strengths
and the principal limitations of the quantitative habitability framework
developed here.

The post-\cassini{} synthesis volumes \textit{Titan After Cassini--Huygens}
(Cambridge University Press, 2025) have distilled the scientific legacy of
the mission.  In the context of surface habitability, the synthesis highlights
three significant gaps: (1) the absence of any quantitative spatially-resolved
habitability probability map of the full globe; (2) the absence of an explicit
multi-epoch temporal treatment connecting the LHB, present, and far-future
habitability regimes; and (3) the absence of a reproducible, openly archived
analysis framework integrating the full suite of Cassini data modalities.
This thesis addresses all three.

\section{The Dragonfly Mission}
\label{sec:dragonfly_intro}

NASA's \dragonfly{} mission \citep{Lorenz2018Dragonfly} was selected as the
fourth New Frontiers programme mission in June 2019.  It is currently en route
to \titan{} with a projected arrival in 2034.  The mission deploys a
dual-quadcopter rotorcraft that exploits \titan{}'s dense atmosphere (surface
density $\rho \approx 5.4$\,kg\,m$^{-3}$) and low gravity to achieve powered
flight, enabling mobility impossible for wheeled rovers.  The baseline
mission plans for $\sim$175\,km of total traverse distance over a 2.7-year
nominal mission, beginning in the Belet dune field to the north-west of Selk
crater and progressing through the crater ejecta blanket to the crater floor.

The scientific rationale for Selk as the primary target rests on the
exposure of water-ice-rich crustal material in the ejecta (enabling Strecker
amino acid synthesis when contacted by melt water), the adjacent organic-rich
dune terrain, and the radar-bright crater rim morphology consistent with
ancient liquid-water exposure.  \dragonfly{} carries a mass spectrometer
(DraMS), a gamma-ray and neutron spectrometer (DraGNS), a drill, a camera
suite (DragonCam), and a meteorology package.  The analysis in this thesis
provides a quantitative evaluation of the mission's site selection and
identifies which habitability features each instrument is best positioned
to test.

\section{Scope and Research Questions}
\label{sec:scope}

Several preceding studies have attempted to place planetary habitability on
a quantitative footing.  The \textit{Earth Similarity Index}
\citep{Schulze-Makuch2011} scores planetary bodies against Earth on
temperature, density, escape velocity, and surface chemistry, but
produces a single scalar per body and cannot represent spatial or temporal
variation.  \citet{Hendrix2019} reviewed the habitability potential of
icy ocean worlds using qualitative multi-criteria matrices, providing rich
scientific context but no spatial resolution or probabilistic framework.
Logistic regression and machine-learning habitability classifiers
(e.g.\ \citealt{Spiegel2012}) have been applied to exoplanet populations
but require labelled training data that does not exist for Titan.
The Bayesian approach adopted here differs from all of these: it encodes
scientific prior knowledge as probability distributions, updates them
with observational data via conjugate updating, and returns credible
intervals rather than point estimates --- making uncertainty explicit
rather than implicit.  The choice of a Beta-conjugate (Beta-Binomial)
model is motivated by the bounded $[0,1]$ domain of habitability
probability and by the analytical tractability that permits simultaneous
evaluation at all 6.49 million grid pixels without numerical integration
or MCMC.  A Gaussian process or multivariate logistic regression could
in principle capture feature correlations more richly, but would require
either strong covariance priors or prohibitively dense labelled sampling
to estimate the correlation structure from Cassini data alone.  The
Beta-conjugate approach is preferred as the most tractable framework
that preserves physical interpretability at every pixel.

Previous habitability assessments of \titan{} have shared four important
limitations.  They are predominantly qualitative or point-specific, lacking
global spatial resolution.  They treat the present epoch as the only relevant
state, ignoring both the LHB prebiotic-chemistry window and the far-future
red-giant window.  They do not integrate the full suite of Cassini data
modalities into a unified probabilistic framework.  And they lack the
reproducibility afforded by an openly archived analysis pipeline.

The closest methodological precedent is \citet{Affholder2021}, who applied
Bayesian statistical inference to Cassini INMS plume data from Enceladus,
comparing the observed methane and hydrogen escape rates against models of
abiotic serpentinisation and biotic methanogenesis.  Their finding --- that
the observed methane escape rate is most parsimoniously explained by
biological methanogenesis if the prior probability of life is sufficient ---
represents the most quantitatively rigorous Bayesian biosignature assessment
yet published for a solar system body.  The present work is directly inspired
by this approach, extended from a single-point plume observation to a full-globe,
multi-feature, multi-epoch spatial mapping.

The specific research questions addressed are:
\begin{description}
  \item[RQ1] \textit{What is the spatial distribution of surface habitability
    on present-day \titan{}, and which terrain types, geological features,
    and atmospheric zones produce the highest scores?  Are the data-derived
    feature importances consistent with the physically motivated prior weights?}

  \item[RQ2] \textit{How did habitability differ during the Late Heavy
    Bombardment, when impact-melt ponds provided transient liquid water?
    Which impact structures were the most astrobiologically significant,
    and what are the implications for prebiotic chemistry?}

  \item[RQ3] \textit{How does habitability evolve through the temporal sequence
    from the LHB to the red-giant future?  Is \titan{} potentially the most
    habitable body in the solar system during the red-giant ocean window?}

  \item[RQ4] \textit{Does the derived habitability map independently validate
    the \dragonfly{} landing site at Selk crater, and which instruments
    are best positioned to test the model's predictions?}
\end{description}

Chapter~\ref{ch:methods} presents the data sources, feature extraction,
Bayesian inference model, and temporal methodology.
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} presents and interprets the habitability maps at
each epoch, the feature importances, the temporal evolution, and the
detailed Selk crater analysis.
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the \dragonfly{} mission implications,
biosignature framework, limitations, and future work.
